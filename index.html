Cracking the "Operations Engineering" gap in India.

01 — Context

What Abstrabit is actually building

While reading about Abstrabit, I noticed something that kept repeating across your website. None of your work starts with AI. It starts with operations. Whether it's workflow automation, AI agents, or internal tools, the common pattern isn't "build AI." It's removing operational friction. That distinction is what made me interested in applying.

Many fast-growing businesses still depend on undocumented processes, shared spreadsheets, chat messages, and the experience of a few key people. As they grow, those informal systems become bottlenecks.

02 — Field Research

What I saw working closely with the school system

From the vantage point of a full-stack web developer embedded within an educational institution, the disconnect between software capability and operational reality was glaring.

I watched teachers buried under administrative overhead, managing attendance on physical registers while generic software sat unused. The problem wasn't a lack of digitization; it was a failure to map software to the messy, human reality of daily operations.

03 — The Gap

Two kinds of companies. Abstrabit is talking to one.

We are looking at two distinct organizational models. Type 1 is tech-forward, adopting SaaS tools naturally. They are well-served.

Type 2 is the fast-growing, founder-led SMB. They are scaling rapidly on sheer operational grit and manual processes. They don't need another point solution; they need an orchestration layer that standardizes their chaos.

04 — Observation

Something I noticed about operations

Traditional tools are passive; they wait for user input. The real paradigm shift is moving from passive repositories to active agents.

When I built LetterAlchemy, the goal wasn't just to format text; it was to actively generate structured communication from fragmented inputs. Operations require systems that pre-empt needs, automatically structuring data the moment it enters the system.

05 — Strategy

What I'd do: The Pilot as a Sales Tool

If I had to approach founder-led SMBs, I wouldn't start by selling software. I'd start by mapping manual handoffs. Every repeated WhatsApp message, spreadsheet copy, approval chain, or missed follow-up represents hidden operational cost. The workflow audit becomes both discovery and product design.

06 — Execution

What I bring: The engineering work, specifically.

I operate across the stack: TypeScript, React, and Node.js. But more importantly, I take architectural ownership.

In building LetterAlchemy and Lucid Finance, I focused on creating robust data pipelines and intuitive interfaces that reduce cognitive load. I build for operational resilience, ensuring systems handle edge cases gracefully.
